Years ago, I created intellectual property for what is known as the Benford’s Wallet. It is designed to ensure that payments cannot be easily traced or linked to one another by employing Benford’s Law. The key to this system is that it splits transaction amounts into varied coins, making them statistically difficult to attribute to a single payment. For example, if you were to pay $100, rather than paying with uniform coins or equal amounts, you would break this payment into multiple coin denominations. These values could range from fractions of a cent to amounts equivalent to a $20 note. This distribution would follow a natural pattern, closely mimicking real-world spending habits, ensuring that the mixed values cannot be easily tracked or identified as part of the same payment.

This idea directly counters the vulnerabilities found in systems like Monero. Monero claims to offer anonymity, but the distinction here is critical: anonymity is not privacy. Anonymity may obscure the identity of the sender, but it does not protect the transaction itself from analysis. Monero has been traceable for years using statistical methods. The design of Monero, which focuses on obscuring transactions without genuinely protecting them from advanced statistical analysis, has allowed many of its transactions to be traced, revealing the limits of its supposed anonymity.

The Benford’s Wallet takes a fundamentally different approach. By leveraging Benford’s Law, it addresses not just the need for anonymity but for actual privacy. Instead of merely masking the identity of a sender, it renders the entire transaction process statistically opaque. No matter how much statistical analysis is applied, the distribution of amounts across varied denominations prevents the matching or tracing of payments. Monero, by contrast, has relied on ring signatures and stealth addresses, but these mechanisms have proven inadequate when faced with the scrutiny of determined analysis. What this means in practice is that while Monero claims to offer anonymity, those promises are hollow—transactions remain vulnerable.
The genius of the Benford’s Wallet is that it embraces the natural patterns that occur in real-world transactions. In reality, people do not make payments in uniform, predictable amounts. By mimicking this natural randomness, the wallet makes it virtually impossible to link transactions together. The natural distribution pattern based on Benford’s Law ensures that no two payments appear related, even when they are part of the same transaction. This is critical for maintaining true privacy. Statistical matching, which is commonly used to track transactions across various systems, becomes ineffective when faced with such randomness.


Monero’s flaws are compounded by its dependence on fixed anonymity sets. While these sets may obscure the identity of participants in a transaction, they do nothing to protect the transaction itself from analysis. The more transactions pass through Monero, the easier it becomes for analysts to identify patterns, trace coins, and reveal the transaction history. It’s a system built on the illusion of security—once you peer behind the curtain, it’s clear that the anonymity is fragile and can be shattered with the right tools. Monero’s dependence on its anonymity features has, in many cases, led to coins being traced and individuals identified despite the system’s promises.


On the other hand, the Benford’s Wallet does not rely on masking or obfuscation. Its strength lies in the natural distribution of amounts that make up each payment. When you break a $100 payment into numerous smaller amounts, each of those amounts follows a pattern that would naturally occur in a typical transaction. By avoiding uniform amounts and embracing this distribution, the wallet ensures that no statistical method can link the payments back to the original sender or even determine that they are part of the same transaction.


This difference is crucial in understanding the gap between what Monero offers and what the Benford’s Wallet provides. Monero’s anonymity relies on outdated methods that have already been compromised, while the Benford’s Wallet guarantees privacy by breaking the transaction process itself into pieces that cannot be put back together. It’s not just about making the sender anonymous—it’s about making the entire payment untraceable. Statistical analysis, which can expose the limitations of systems like Monero, is rendered completely useless when faced with the natural, randomised patterns produced by the Benford’s Wallet.


Furthermore, the Benford’s Wallet places the power back in the hands of the user. The beauty of the system is that it allows for flexibility. If you want a more private transaction, you can split the payment into as many coins as you desire. You can decide how much privacy you need. If you’re willing to pay slightly more for that additional level of privacy, you can do so by breaking your payment into smaller, more varied amounts. Some merchants may not care about receiving payments in this manner, while others might prefer larger sums. But the decision remains with the individual—there is no centralised control dictating how private your transactions must be.


This stands in stark contrast to Monero, where users are at the mercy of a flawed system that offers only the illusion of privacy. Monero’s reliance on outdated anonymity features means that users are not in control of their own privacy. The system dictates the terms, and those terms are far from secure. The Benford’s Wallet, by giving users the ability to control how their transactions are structured, empowers them to decide for themselves how much privacy they need.


Monero’s fundamental flaw is its reliance on a concept of anonymity that is inherently limited. Anonymity alone is not enough to protect users in the face of modern statistical analysis. The Benford’s Wallet, by ensuring that transaction amounts are varied and naturally distributed, guarantees true privacy. No statistical analysis can break the system, because the system itself is built to emulate the randomness of real-world behaviour.


The Benford’s Wallet offers a solution to the vulnerabilities that plague Monero and other systems that rely solely on anonymity. By leveraging Benford’s Law, it ensures that payments cannot be traced or linked together, rendering statistical matching useless. Monero, which has been traceable for years despite its claims of anonymity, simply cannot offer the same level of privacy. With the Benford’s Wallet, the user is in control, deciding how much privacy they need and how they want their transactions to appear. It’s a system that puts the individual first, offering true privacy in an age where that is becoming increasingly rare.
